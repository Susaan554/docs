After AI Resume Analyzer processes your resume, it generates a structured report packed with insights about your document's strengths, weaknesses, and opportunities for improvement. This guide explains what each section means and how to use the findings to take meaningful action.

## Report Overview

Your report is organized into clearly labeled sections so you can navigate directly to what matters most. Here's what you'll find:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Resume Summary" icon="file-lines">
    A one-line AI-generated summary of your resume — useful for quickly confirming the AI understood your background and experience correctly.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Overall Score" icon="star">
    A quality score representing how well your resume is written, structured, and optimized. Use this as your baseline before making any edits.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Strengths" icon="thumbs-up">
    The sections, skills, and achievements the AI rated positively. These are the parts of your resume you should keep and build upon.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Weaknesses" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Areas the AI flagged for improvement. Each weakness comes with context explaining why it was identified and what you can do about it.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Skill Gaps" icon="chart-bar">
    Skills present in the job description that are absent or underrepresented in your resume. This section only appears when you run an analysis in **Job Match** mode.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Match Score" icon="percent">
    Your resume's compatibility percentage with the target job description. A higher score means stronger alignment with the role's requirements. Appears in **Job Match** mode only.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Course Recommendations" icon="graduation-cap">
    Curated learning resources suggested by the AI to help you close your identified skill gaps. Prioritize courses that address your top missing skills.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Using the Report to Improve

Work through your report in this order to make the highest-impact improvements first.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Start with your Overall Score">
    Your **Overall Score** gives you an immediate snapshot of where your resume stands. Note this number — you'll use it as a benchmark to measure the impact of your edits after you re-run the analysis.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review your Strengths">
    Read through the **Strengths** section to understand what the AI rated highly. Protect these elements as you revise your resume and look for ways to reinforce or expand on them in other sections.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Address each item in Weaknesses">
    The **Weaknesses** section contains your highest-impact improvement opportunities. Work through each flagged item one at a time — even fixing two or three issues can meaningfully raise your score.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review Skill Gaps and prioritize top missing skills">
    If you ran a **Job Match** analysis, open the **Skill Gaps** section and identify the skills that appear most prominently in the job description. Add these to your resume where you genuinely have experience with them, and note the others as learning priorities.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Check Course Recommendations">
    Browse the **Course Recommendations** to find learning resources that address your most critical gaps. Enroll in the courses most relevant to your target role and timeline.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Update your resume and re-run the analysis">
    After making edits, re-upload your updated resume and run a fresh analysis. Compare your new **Overall Score** and **Match Score** against your earlier baseline to measure your progress.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Common Report Findings

If you're seeing certain findings repeatedly, here's how to address them effectively.

<Accordion title="Missing Professional Summary">
  A professional summary is a 2–4 sentence paragraph at the top of your resume that frames your experience, highlights your strongest skills, and states the type of role you're targeting. To add one, place it directly below your contact information. Write in first person implied (no "I"), lead with your years of experience and field, mention your top two or three strengths, and close with your career goal. Example: *"Results-driven software engineer with 5 years of experience building scalable web applications. Proficient in React, Node.js, and cloud infrastructure. Seeking a senior engineering role at a product-led company."*
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Weak Action Verbs">
  Passive or vague language — such as "responsible for," "helped with," or "worked on" — undercuts the impact of your experience. Replace these with strong, specific action verbs that convey ownership and results. For example: replace *"Responsible for managing the deployment pipeline"* with *"Architected and maintained the CI/CD deployment pipeline."* Strong action verbs include: led, built, designed, reduced, launched, negotiated, streamlined, and drove.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Lack of Quantified Achievements">
  Bullet points that describe duties without numbers fail to show the scale or impact of your work. Wherever possible, add metrics: percentages, dollar amounts, time saved, team sizes, or volume. For example: *"Improved application load time by 40% by refactoring the data-fetching layer"* is far more compelling than *"Improved application performance."* If you don't have exact figures, use reasonable estimates and frame them with phrases like "approximately" or "reduced by roughly."
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Missing Keywords">
  Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) scan resumes for keywords that match the job description before a human ever reads them. If your resume lacks the specific terms used in job postings — such as technology names, methodologies, or role-specific jargon — it may be filtered out automatically. To fix this, run a **Job Match** analysis against your target job description, review the **Skill Gaps** section, and naturally incorporate the missing keywords into your experience bullets and skills section where they accurately reflect your background.
</Accordion>

<Tip>
  Save or screenshot your report before making any edits to your resume. Having a record of your before scores lets you make a clear before-and-after comparison when you re-run the analysis after your revisions.
</Tip>
